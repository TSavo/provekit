# After Cross-Language: Every Cross-X Boundary Dissolves the Same Way

*Paper 17 closed the cross-language thread: every language is a dialect over one shared algebra, and the renaming morphisms between dialects are content-addressed discharged facts. Paper 18 named the concept hub and the three-key composition that puts cross-language transport into developer hands. Paper 19 made the concepts first-class catalog entries. Paper 20 wrote the unification theorem. This paper says one more thing, which the prior four implicitly already said and which is worth saying explicitly because it changes what an engineer hears the substrate offers. The mechanism that dissolved cross-language dissolves every cross-X. Not by analogy. By the same mechanism, with the same math, applied to a different axis. Cross-library, cross-framework, cross-runtime, cross-platform, cross-protocol, cross-database, cross-architecture, cross-version: each is structurally a cross-language problem, and each terminates in the same colimit through the same hub.*

## 0. The claim

For any axis X along which two software artifacts differ but mean-the-same — language, library, framework, runtime, architecture, protocol, database engine, version — the mechanism that makes them commensurable is one mechanism. Lift each artifact to ProofIR. Recognize equivalent `(V, A, C, ≤)` tuples by their CIDs. Emit either side from the shared substrate. The work to support N artifacts on one axis is M+N (M lifters in, N realizers out), not M×N. For K axes simultaneously, the work is the sum over k of M_k+N_k, not the product. Cross-X scales linearly per axis and additively across axes. The substrate does not grow per axis. The concept catalog grows. The routing is uniform.

This is what paper 18 said about cross-language, lifted from "language" to "any X." The lift is free because the argument never used anything specific about language. Two surfaces, one substrate, one hub: the hub doesn't know whether the surface is Java or React or PostgreSQL or x86-64. It knows the surface declares a `(V, A, C, ≤)` tuple and the tuple has a CID.

## 1. What a cross-X boundary actually is

A "cross-X" boundary in software is, on inspection, always the same shape. Two artifacts in two surfaces. Each surface has a syntax for *expressing* what the artifact means. Each surface has its own conventions, sugar, ecosystem assumptions, and lossiness. The artifacts mean-the-same iff there is some shared object both surfaces are presentations of. The "boundary" is the human work of recognizing that shared object and writing the translation by hand.

This shape is invariant in X. The cross-language case has it (Java's `Optional<T>` and Rust's `Option<T>` are surfaces over `concept:option`). The cross-library case has it (`lodash.isEqual` and `ramda.equals` are surfaces over `concept:deep-equality`). The cross-framework case has it (React's `useState` and Vue's `ref` are surfaces over `concept:reactive-cell`). The cross-runtime case has it (`async fn` over tokio and `async function` over Node both surface `concept:async-task` with different scheduler semantics). The cross-database case has it (SQL `JOIN` and MongoDB's `$lookup` are surfaces over `concept:relational-join`, partially: there are loss-records on each side for what the other can't express). The cross-protocol case has it (gRPC, JSON-RPC, MessagePack-RPC are surfaces over `concept:remote-procedure-call` and `concept:wire-encoding`). The cross-architecture case has it (`vmovups` on x86 and `ld1` on ARM are surfaces over `concept:packed-vector-load`). The cross-version case has it (Python 2 `print x` and Python 3 `print(x)` are surfaces over `concept:write-line`).

The "boundary" is the same primitive shape every time. Two presentations, one referent, the referent is unstated, and the human work is to state it. The substrate's move — also the same every time — is to *name* the referent with a CID and let the surfaces both point to it. Once both surfaces point to the same CID, the boundary is no longer a boundary. It is a coreference.

## 2. The mechanism never used a property of language

Re-read papers 12 through 17 with the language word excised and a placeholder X put in its place. Every line still goes through. ProofIR represents every X. The X-ports are content-addressed mementos. Cross-X verification is composition of renaming morphisms. ProofIR is universal because it forgets the X-specific texture and keeps the X-independent obligation. The substrate is closed under cross-X composition because the morphisms compose. The address of an operation was always a mathematical fact independent of X.

This is the load-bearing observation. The mechanism in those papers never used a property of language. It used the property of *surface-ness*: a presentation, lossy, syntactically specific, with idiomatic conventions, over a structurally invariant referent. Languages are surfaces. So are libraries, frameworks, runtimes, protocols, databases, architectures, versions. The argument is *about surfaces over a substrate*. Language was just the most familiar example.

Once you see that, "the substrate supports cross-language" stops being the headline and becomes a corollary. The headline is: the substrate is the hub for *every* surface-over-substrate dissolution. Cross-language was the case the literature had words for. Cross-everything-else is the rest of the case, with the same math.

## 3. The catalog of dissolutions

Make the list concrete. For each X-axis below, the surfaces are listed, the concept-hub binding is named, and the loss-records describe what doesn't transport cleanly. Read the list as: "this is what the catalog looks like once it has been populated."

**Cross-library.** `lodash.isEqual`, `ramda.equals`, `underscore.isEqual`, `fast-deep-equal`, the hand-rolled `function deepEqual(a, b) { ... }` in every codebase, Java `Objects.deepEquals`, Python `==` (for hashable structures), C++ `operator==` (with an `==` defined for every member type, recursively). All surfaces over `concept:deep-equality`. The loss-records cover edge cases: lodash treats `NaN === NaN` as true; native `===` treats it as false; Python `==` on `float` follows IEEE; C++ on `float` follows IEEE. A function that depends on the loss-distinguished behavior carries a `loss_dim: {nan_equality: "strict"|"reflexive"}` and the realizer for each surface knows whether that surface can express the requested loss-dim. If lodash is the source and native `===` is the target, the realizer emits a hand-rolled deepEqual with the NaN special-case, not a bare `===`. The concept doesn't shrink to the lowest common denominator. The concept carries the union, and each surface emits what it can or refuses with a `lossy-realization-refusal`.

**Cross-framework.** React `useState`, Vue 3 `ref`, Vue 2 reactive option, Svelte 5 `$state`, Solid `createSignal`, MobX `observable`, Knockout `observable`, Backbone `Model.set`, Angular signals, Lit reactive properties. All surfaces over `concept:reactive-cell`. The loss-records cover scheduling semantics (synchronous like Solid, batched like React, microtask like Vue), equality strategies (referential like React, deep for primitives like Svelte, configurable like MobX), subscription lifetime (component-scoped like React, manual like MobX), and reactivity granularity (component-level like React, sub-property-level like Vue/Solid/Svelte). A reactive-cell concept-citation in source compiles, per target framework, to that framework's idiom, with the loss-records discharged either by emitting the matching configuration or by emitting a `lossy-realization-refusal` memento and stopping. Migrating a codebase from React to Solid is no longer a hand-port. It is a re-realize pass.

**Cross-runtime.** Tokio tasks, async-std tasks, Node Promises with the V8 event loop, Bun's bun-uvloop, Deno's Rust event loop, Python asyncio, Go goroutines, Erlang processes. All surfaces over `concept:async-task` with sub-concepts `concept:cooperative-scheduling`, `concept:preemptive-scheduling`, `concept:fiber`, `concept:os-thread`. The loss-records cover cancellation semantics, blocking-call handling, structured-concurrency support, cross-thread vs. single-threaded execution. A coroutine that depends on synchronous cancellation can be realized into tokio (`tokio::select!`) but refuses to realize into early-Node Promise (no cancellation primitive); the refusal carries the `loss_dim: cancellation_required` it could not discharge.

**Cross-architecture.** `addps` (x86 SSE), `vaddps` (x86 AVX), `fadd.4s` (ARM NEON), `vfadd.vv` (RISC-V V), CUDA `__fadd_rn`, SPIR-V `OpFAdd`. All surfaces over `concept:packed-vector-add-f32`. The loss-records cover lane width (SSE is 4, AVX-512 is 16, NEON is 4, RISC-V V is parameterized, CUDA depends on warp), rounding modes (default-round-nearest for the SIMD instructions; explicit for the CUDA intrinsic), exception handling (most SIMD lanes signal flags rather than trap; CUDA traps). A kernel that depends on lane-precise rounding-mode control realizes to x86-AVX-with-mxcsr or to CUDA, refuses to NEON (NEON's rounding is global). This is what cross-arch portability has always *been* in practice; the substrate names it.

**Cross-protocol.** gRPC over HTTP/2, JSON-RPC over WebSocket, MessagePack-RPC over TCP, OpenAPI over HTTP/1, GraphQL over HTTP/1. All surfaces over `concept:remote-procedure-call` × `concept:wire-encoding` × `concept:transport`. The decomposition matters: a service is the colimit of three orthogonal concepts. The loss-records cover schema evolution (gRPC has it, JSON-RPC doesn't), streaming (gRPC has it natively, REST simulates with chunked-encoding), bidi (gRPC, WebSocket, raw TCP), and error model (HTTP status codes vs. gRPC status codes vs. JSON-RPC error objects). A service interface authored as concept citations is dispatchable over any of these by selecting a `transport_realization_plan`.

**Cross-database.** PostgreSQL `JOIN`, MySQL `JOIN`, SQLite `JOIN`, MongoDB `$lookup`, DynamoDB `BatchGetItem` followed by app-side join, Neo4j Cypher pattern-match, RDF SPARQL `?x ?p ?y`, ElasticSearch parent-child. All surfaces over `concept:relational-join` with sub-concepts `concept:equi-join`, `concept:semi-join`, `concept:anti-join`, `concept:theta-join`. The loss-records cover index availability (Postgres has them, DynamoDB doesn't for non-key fields), transactionality (SQL has it, Mongo had to grow it, DynamoDB has it with cost), and ordering semantics. The substrate makes "ports an app from Postgres to DynamoDB" a re-realize over the same concept citations, with refusals where the loss-record exceeds the target's expressive capability.

**Cross-version.** Python 2 `print x`, Python 3 `print(x)`, Python 3 `print(x, file=sys.stderr)`. All surfaces over `concept:write-line`. The version-axis is a special case where M_lifters ≈ M_realizers (you usually have lifters and realizers for both versions of the same language). The migration tool that 2to3 was, by hand, on syntactic patterns, is in the substrate the realize pass from the shared concept catalog into the target-version surface. The same applies to Java 8 → Java 17 `var` and `record` and switch-expressions, ES5 → ES6 `class` and `let` and arrow, C89 → C99 designated initializers. A version bump is a realize.

**Cross-platform.** POSIX `read`/`write`, Win32 `ReadFile`/`WriteFile`, async-completion-port (IOCP), Linux io_uring, BSD kqueue, macOS Grand Central Dispatch. All surfaces over `concept:byte-stream-io` × `concept:completion-model`. Loss-records cover completion model (sync, callback, future, completion-port), error reporting (errno vs. GetLastError vs. NTSTATUS), and capability surface (POSIX is small, Win32 is large). The historical "cross-platform abstraction library" (libuv, asio, gio, glib) is a per-X-axis manual lift; the substrate makes it a catalog lookup.

The list is partial. Add any other "cross-X" you can think of. The same shape will fit: surfaces, a concept hub, loss-records per cell, M+N lift/realize, refusals when the loss exceeds the target. The argument never uses what X is. It uses only that X has surfaces.

## 4. The math is M+N, summed over axes

The naive way to support K cross-X axes with M surfaces per axis is M×M×M×... up to K factors. Translating a Rust+lodash+React+tokio+x86 program to a Python+ramda+Solid+asyncio+ARM program is a 5-axis translation with 2 surfaces per axis. Naively, that's 2^5 = 32 distinct paths, and the engineering bill is 32 hand-written translators.

The substrate's way is per-axis M+N, summed. Lifters per axis: one per surface. Realizers per axis: one per surface. For the 5-axis 2-surface case, that's 5 axes × (2+2) = 20 components; for 5-axis 10-surface, it is 5 × (10+10) = 100, where the naive count is 10^5 = 100,000. The growth is linear per axis and *additive across axes* because the substrate's middle is uniform.

The reason it is additive and not multiplicative is that the substrate's middle does not have to know the K axes interact. A program that uses lodash and React and tokio and x86 is, at the substrate, a `(V, A, C, ≤)` tuple where the operations on lodash routines, React hooks, tokio futures, and x86 intrinsics all sit in the same algebra. Realizing the tuple to Python+ramda+Solid+asyncio+ARM is a per-operation realize that asks per-axis "what is the target's binding for this concept on this axis?" The 5-axis query is 5 independent lookups, not a 5-dimensional table.

This is the substrate's central engineering claim, restated: the catalog is multi-dimensional, but each dimension is indexed independently. The cell `(concept:reactive-cell, framework=Solid)` is one lookup. The cell `(concept:async-task, runtime=asyncio)` is another. They are queried independently. The program assembles them. The combinatorial blowup is dissolved by the address-space's flatness (paper 16): the address is a CID; CIDs have no internal structure; you cannot "combine" two CIDs into a "joint" address. You can only co-cite them.

The consequence is operational, not theoretical: a 5-axis migration costs the substrate the *sum* of the per-axis catalog work, paid once across all migrations that touch those axes. The Java+Spring+Hibernate+JBoss+OpenJDK app moves to Kotlin+Quarkus+Panache+Wildfly+GraalVM at a cost the substrate has already amortized across every other migration that touched any of those five axes. Migration costs collapse from "per-app project" to "per-cell catalog entry," and the catalog is shared across humanity.

## 5. The TS-dev-on-Zig-project scenario, walked through

A concrete consequence is the kind of thing that sounds absurd but is the natural reading of the math. A TypeScript developer can contribute to a Zig project without writing Zig.

The setup. A Zig project has been lifted: every Zig source file has been walked into ProofIR, the algebraic ops have been content-addressed, the contracts have been lifted, the algebra catalog has been populated. The Zig project's `build.zig` and its source CIDs are committed to the proofchain. The project has, in the substrate, a `(V, A, C, ≤)` tuple per function and per module, with a `language: zig` realization edge for each.

The TS dev opens the project. The substrate's editor view *realizes* the Zig source into TypeScript, on demand, by traversing the realize-edge from each Zig source-CID to the TypeScript surface. This is not a transpilation in the historical sense (Zig → JS through emscripten); it is a realize from the substrate's algebra to the TypeScript surface, mediated by the concept hub. The TS dev reads TypeScript, idiomatic, with TypeScript types.

The TS dev edits. They write TypeScript. Their edits are lifted from TypeScript to ProofIR. The lift produces `(V, A, C, ≤)` tuples in the same substrate. The substrate diffs the new tuples against the prior tuples on a per-operation basis; the diff is structural. The substrate then realizes the diff back to Zig (the project's canonical surface). The Zig output is idiomatic Zig: error-union returns where TypeScript had `throw`, `comptime` parameters where TypeScript had generic constraints, `defer` where TypeScript had `try/finally`. The realizer knows the project's stylistic profile from prior commits.

The Zig dev reviews. They see Zig in their editor. The Zig dev's review is "does this Zig do what I want?" They do not need to learn TypeScript. They never see the TypeScript surface. The CI gate runs: did the lift's contract diff discharge cleanly? did the realize round-trip with no loss-records the project's policy forbids? did the TS dev's program satisfy the existing Zig project's catalog of concept citations? If yes, the change merges. The git history is a Zig diff. The proofchain head records that the change was authored over the TypeScript surface and realized onto the Zig surface; both surfaces are first-class.

The scenario is not magic. It is what the math predicts: cross-language is cross-X with X = language, and the substrate dissolves cross-X uniformly. The thing that has to be true for this to work is that *both surfaces have been lifted into the substrate*. The TypeScript surface needs a lifter and a realizer; the Zig surface needs a lifter and a realizer; the algebra has to contain both surfaces' operations as morphisms over the concept hub. None of this is per-project work. It is one-time per-surface, and once the catalog has both, every Zig project becomes contribute-able from TypeScript and every TypeScript project becomes contribute-able from Zig. M+N, summed across all (language, language) pairs.

The same scenario runs for cross-framework (a React dev contributing to a Vue project), cross-runtime (a tokio dev contributing to an asyncio project), cross-architecture (an x86 SIMD dev contributing to an ARM NEON kernel), cross-database (a Postgres dev contributing to a Mongo-backed app). The pattern is identical. The substrate is the editor of the user's choice.

## 6. Authored, inferred, generated: three paths to a concept binding

The catalog has to be populated. There are three ways a binding from a surface artifact to a concept gets into the catalog, and each has a different trust profile.

**Authored.** A human declares, by writing a memento, that `surface:lodash.isEqual` and `concept:deep-equality` are the same. The memento is a `concept-binding-claim`. It carries a signature. It is content-addressed. The substrate accepts it the way it accepts any signed claim; whether the verifier trusts it depends on the local policy. Authored bindings are the bedrock of the catalog. They are how the GoF book (paper 19) is translated into substrate: a human points at the pattern and names it.

**Inferred.** The substrate, given two surface artifacts and the lifted `(V, A, C, ≤)` tuples for each, can *recognize* that the tuples are structurally equivalent up to renaming. The structural-equivalence check is a CID comparison after a renaming morphism is applied; the morphism is content-addressed; the equivalence claim is a discharged fact, not an assertion. Inferred bindings are how the catalog grows automatically: as lifters process more code, more pairs of "surface A and surface B independently named the same thing" surface, and the substrate proposes a binding. The human still has to accept or reject; the inferred binding is a candidate with a discharge proof, not a fait accompli.

**Generated.** A language model proposes a binding. The model has read the docs for lodash and the docs for ramda; it proposes that `lodash.isEqual` and `ramda.equals` are surfaces over the same concept. The model's proposal becomes a `concept-binding-claim` with `source: llm-generated` and an explicit confidence basis. The substrate then runs the discharge: it lifts both surfaces and checks the structural equivalence. If the equivalence holds, the claim is accepted; if it doesn't, the claim is refused with a `lossy-realization-refusal` carrying the precise structural divergence. Generated bindings are how the catalog scales beyond what humans can hand-author: the model is a candidate-generator, the substrate is a candidate-discharger, and the bindings that survive are exactly the ones a human author would have produced by hand if they had had time. Authored / inferred / generated, with discharge.

The three paths all end as content-addressed claims. The substrate doesn't care which path produced the claim. The verifier's local policy may weight authored > inferred > generated, or the opposite, or none of the above. The catalog is the union of all three.

## 7. What this does not solve

The dissolution mechanism is not omnipotent. It dissolves a specific class of work: the work of *translating* between surfaces of the same meaning. It does not dissolve work that is not translation.

It does not dissolve **architectural disagreement.** Two developers can agree on every concept binding and disagree about whether the program should use an actor model, a request-response model, or an event-sourced model. The substrate makes their disagreement crisp — they are choosing different `concept:execution-model` bindings — but it does not pick a winner. Architecture is taste, expressed as concept selection, and the substrate makes the selection visible without resolving it.

It does not dissolve **performance characteristics.** Two realizations of the same concept can have different time and space costs. `concept:hash-map` realized to `std::unordered_map`, `tsl::robin_map`, `absl::flat_hash_map`, or a hand-rolled open-addressed table have wildly different cache behavior. The substrate makes the loss-record explicit (some realizations don't support stable iterators; some support custom hashers; some have specific memory-allocation profiles); the choice between them is a performance budget, not a correctness one. Performance is taste-with-numbers, and the substrate measures the numbers but does not pick the winner.

It does not dissolve **idiomaticity.** Two realizations can be byte-different but semantically equivalent. `for (let i = 0; i < arr.length; i++)` and `arr.forEach(...)` and `for (const x of arr)` are all surfaces over `concept:linear-iteration`, and a project's style guide may say which one to emit. The substrate emits *one*; the choice is the project's policy, content-addressed as a `realization_policy_memento`. Idiomaticity is style, and the substrate makes style content-addressed.

It does not dissolve **cultural alignment.** Two ecosystems can agree on every concept binding and disagree on which problems matter. The Rust ecosystem cares about zero-cost abstractions and memory-safety-without-GC; the Erlang ecosystem cares about let-it-crash and hot-code-reload. Both express their values in concept citations, but the values are not in the substrate. The substrate makes the values comparable; it does not unify them.

It does not dissolve **domain expertise.** A developer who understands the substrate but does not understand cryptography cannot write good cryptography by realize-from-some-other-language. The cryptography is in the concept citations, and the substrate makes the citations cross-language, but if the citations are wrong the realize is wrong. Domain knowledge is the load-bearing input; the substrate is the load-bearing transport. They are not the same thing.

This list is the answer to the question "what is left for humans to do?" Architecture, performance budgets, style, cultural values, domain knowledge. The dissolution mechanism takes the *mechanical* work of cross-X translation off the human's plate. It does not take the *substantive* work off. The substantive work was always the work that mattered. The mechanical work was always the friction tax.

## 8. The phase transition: language as accessibility, not gate

A phase transition is what happens when a quantitative change in the substrate produces a qualitative change in the world above it. The substrate's quantitative change is "cross-X cost goes from M×N to M+N, additively across X-axes." The qualitative change above is several things at once:

**Language becomes an accessibility feature, not a gate.** Hiring stops conditioning on "knows Zig" and starts conditioning on "knows the domain." A team picks the language each member is most fluent in; the substrate handles the rest. The historical practice of "the codebase is in language X, so everyone working on it must learn X" becomes archaic. The codebase is in the substrate; the languages are views.

**Library becomes a sugar choice, not a lock-in.** A project starts with lodash; later it moves to ramda; later it moves to native ES2025; each move is a realize-pass against the same concept citations. The historical practice of "this codebase is too locked-in to migrate" becomes archaic. The codebase is in the substrate; the libraries are surfaces.

**Framework becomes a render mode, not an architecture.** A project starts with React; the company experiments with Solid; the team prefers Svelte; each migration is a realize-pass. The historical practice of "we are a React shop" becomes a statement about realize defaults, not a statement about architecture. The architecture is in the concept citations.

**Runtime becomes a target, not a destiny.** A service starts on Node; it moves to Bun for the startup-time; it moves to Deno for the security model; it moves to Workerd for the deployment shape; each move is a realize. The historical practice of "rewriting the service in Rust" becomes "selecting a different runtime for the same substrate."

**Architecture-the-cpu-kind becomes an effect handler, not a religion.** A kernel targets x86; it targets ARM; it targets RISC-V; it targets a GPU; each targets a different lane-width and a different scheduler and a different memory model. The historical practice of "porting to ARM" becomes a realize pass with the loss-records made explicit.

**Database engine becomes a query plan, not a commitment.** An app uses Postgres; it experiments with DuckDB for analytics; it tries CockroachDB for geo-replication; it ports to SQLite for the embedded case; each port is a realize. The historical practice of "we chose Postgres five years ago and now we are stuck with it" becomes an artifact of the era before cross-database transport.

**Version becomes a coordinate, not a migration project.** A Python 3.8 codebase becomes a Python 3.12 codebase by realize-pass; the codebase is in the substrate, the version is the realize target. The historical practice of "the Python 2 to Python 3 migration took ten years" becomes a story about a world that did not have a substrate.

The phase transition has a single shape. Every "X is the thing the codebase is in" becomes "X is the surface the codebase is *currently being projected onto*." The codebase is in the substrate. The surfaces are choices. The choices are reversible. The mechanical work of changing choices is the realize pass, paid once per surface, amortized across humanity.

## 9. The shortest version

Cross-language was the case the literature had words for. Cross-anything-else has the same math. The substrate already had the mechanism; this paper says explicitly that the mechanism is uniform, that the catalog is multi-dimensional and additively indexed, and that the consequence is a phase transition in which surfaces stop gating contribution. The TypeScript developer who never wrote Zig but contributed to the Zig project is not a thought experiment. It is the natural reading of papers 12 through 20, and once the catalog has the bindings, it is the default mode of cross-team contribution.

The substrate does not get bigger to support new X-axes. The concept catalog grows linearly in the number of cells `(concept, surface_on_axis_X)`. The routing is uniform: lift to ProofIR, look up the concept binding for the target surface, realize. The math says M+N. The catalog says how many cells. The phase transition says what the world looks like once the catalog is populated. We are, currently, partway through populating it. Each new lifter and realizer adds a row or a column. The substrate does not change.

Cross-X is one observation. It has been hiding inside "cross-language" the whole time. Naming it is this paper.
