# After Babel: We Speak in Vectors Now

*This paper is the capstone of the language thread. Paper 12 made algorithms content-addressed and showed ProofIR represents every language. Paper 13 made the languages themselves content-addressed algebras. Paper 16 named the universal object: the algebra is the portable thing, each element an artifact, the address space the colimit of every lift diagram. This paper draws the conclusion those three were converging on. The conclusion is not about software. It is about naming, about what a programming language is, and about what work we are actually doing when we lift, compose, and discharge. We are not cataloguing the points of a vast library. We are mapping the paths through it. And the names we map by are vectors.*

## 0. The claim

Every programming language you have ever written is a dialect. There is one language underneath. Its words are vectors. We are not building it; it was always there. We are not even discovering its points one by one; the points were always there. We are surveying and signposting the *paths* through it, one route at a time, and the signposts are content identifiers.

This paper assembles the claim from the prior art it has been waiting for: Leibniz's *characteristica universalis* and characteristic numbers; Frege's distinction of sense from reference; Hoare's axiomatic triples and Liskov's representation-independent abstract data types; Dijkstra's predicate transformers; Lawvere's functorial semantics and Goguen's institutions; the Curry-Howard-Lambek correspondence; Cousot's abstract interpretation; Merkle's hash trees and the content-addressed-storage lineage; Herbrand's universe of finite ground terms; Gödel's numbering of syntax; and Borges's library. Each of these is half of the move. Content-addressing applied to *meaning* rather than to bytes is the other half. Put together, they say: programming languages are presentations of one algebra; the algebra's elements have intrinsic addresses; the addresses are computed, not assigned; and the substrate's work is to chart the route network among them.

## 1. Leibniz's program, and the canonicalization function he was missing

Leibniz, in the *Dissertatio de arte combinatoria* (1666) and across the next forty years, wanted a *characteristica universalis*: a symbolic language in which every concept decomposes into primitives, every primitive carries a symbol, and reasoning becomes calculation, so that a dispute between two thinkers could be settled the way a dispute between two arithmeticians is settled. *Calculemus.* Let us compute.

He went further than the dream. Around 1679 he proposed *characteristic numbers*: assign a prime to each primitive concept, let composite concepts be the products of their primitives' numbers, and let "S is P" hold exactly when the number of S is divisible by the number of P. Entailment becomes divisibility. This is the right *shape*. Identify the primitive by a *computed value*. Compose by an operation on values. Make entailment a *relation* on values. He was wrong only about the value: primes do not scale, and a prime-product encoding presupposes that every concept is a finite conjunction of independent features, which is false of the full algebra of computation (control flow, binding, effects, and recursion are not conjunctions of features).

What Leibniz had was the calculus of reasoning and the idea of identifying primitives by a number. What he lacked was a *canonicalization function*: a computable normal form for arbitrary structure such that "two things are the same thing" reduces to "two things have the same canonical bytes." JSON canonicalization (RFC 8785) composed with a cryptographic hash is that function. It accepts arbitrary structure, not just conjunctions of features, because the structure is serialized whole. It is computable, deterministic, and total. And the symbol-is-its-hash move closes the metalanguage gap by deleting the metalanguage: every prior formal system needed a fixed interpretation supplied by an outer language, and the outer language was a human artifact with its own slack, all the way down. With content-addressing there is no level below the bytes. The if-operation is not the word "if" and not a position in a numbering scheme some authority chose; it is `blake3-512:402feb91...`, the hash of its canonical structure, which carries its arity, its result sort, its weakest-precondition rule, and its place in the C11 signature, and which two parties who never coordinated will both compute. Leibniz had the dream and the calculus. He was waiting on a hash function.

One distinction the prior art makes us draw. Content-addressing is not new. Merkle's hash trees (1979), Git (2005), IPFS (2014) all name *byte strings* by the hash of their content. The move here is to name *meanings* by the hash of their *canonical structure*: an operation, a contract, a proof, a derivation. The boundary projection that turns a term into its contract (paper 9) and the deterministic serialization that turns structure into canonical bytes (the canonicalizer) are the work that makes a hash function into a *meaning*-identity function. Naming bytes by hash is forty-five years old. Naming the semantic object by hash, so that semantic identity reduces to byte identity, is the new bit.

## 2. The symbol is the reference; the dialect is the sense

Frege, in *Über Sinn und Bedeutung* (1892), separated the *sense* (Sinn) of a name from its *reference* (Bedeutung). "Morning star" and "evening star" have different senses and the same reference: Venus. The sense is the mode of presentation; the reference is the thing.

A content identifier is pure reference. `blake3-512:402feb91...` is the if-operation with no sense attached: no syntax, no convenience, no mnemonic, nothing to grasp. Every programming language, every proof assistant, every instruction set provides a *sense* for it: C renders it `if`, Rust renders it `if`, Lean renders it `ite`, a term renderer renders it `if(cond, then, else)`, an x86 backend renders it `cmp; jcc`. These are different senses of the same reference. "Every programming language is a dialect" is exactly this: a dialect supplies the senses, the reference is shared, and the dialects are commensurable because the references coincide whatever sounds you make. A renaming morphism in the catalog is the discharged proof that two senses have the same reference: `c11:if ↦ shape:if`, witnessed, content-addressed. Cross-language verification is composition of these coreference proofs.

A content identifier is also, in Kripke's sense (*Naming and Necessity*, 1972), a *rigid designator*: it picks out the same thing in every possible world, because it is the hash of the structure that makes that thing what it is. There is no world in which `blake3-512:402feb91...` is some other operation, the way "the inventor of bifocals" might in some world have been someone other than Franklin. The CID does not designate via a description that could have applied to something else; it designates via the content. This is why federation works without coordination and why the identity relation it induces is the bedrock one: "two things, same CID, therefore the same thing" is not a convention to be trusted but a cryptographic fact to be broken or accepted.

## 3. Contracts are triples; signatures are algebras; substitutability is a discharged path

The contract a function carries is a Hoare triple (Hoare, "An Axiomatic Basis for Computer Programming," 1969; Floyd, "Assigning Meanings to Programs," 1967): a precondition, a body, a postcondition. A `function-contract` memento is a Hoare triple with a content identifier. Nothing in the substrate's notion of a contract is novel; what is novel is that the triple has an address and so participates in a content-addressed graph.

The signature a language carries is an abstract data type in Liskov's sense (Liskov and Zilles, "Programming with Abstract Data Types," 1974; the algebraic-specification tradition that followed, Guttag and Horning 1978, the ADJ group's initial-algebra semantics). An ADT is its operations and the axioms relating them, independent of representation: an *algebra*. A `LanguageSignatureMemento` is Liskov's ADT given an address: sorts, operations, equations, effect signatures, content-addressed, federated. Categorially this is a Lawvere theory (Lawvere, "Functorial Semantics of Algebraic Theories," 1963) presented in an institution (Goguen and Burstall, "Institutions: Abstract Model Theory for Specification and Programming," 1992): a language signature is a presentation in some institution, and the substrate is that institution made content-addressed. Paper 13 made the languages algebras; the institution framing is what lets the substrate host *many* logics at once, each a presentation, each commensurable with the others through morphisms.

And the Liskov Substitution Principle (Liskov and Wing, "A Behavioral Notion of Subtyping," 1994) is, in this setting, not a design rule a programmer is exhorted to obey but a *discharge condition* on a path. A subtype substitutes for a supertype exactly when the subtype's renderings satisfy the supertype's contracts; a dialect "is a" presentation of a shape exactly when the dialect's renderings satisfy the shape's contracts. That is precisely what a renaming morphism, or a refinement-claim memento, *discharges*. The substrate makes the LSP checkable at planet scale by content-address, rather than asserted by a programmer and trusted by a reviewer. Substitutability becomes a verified edge.

## 4. Why the address is a hash, and why the lossiness is the point

A hash is a one-way compression: 512 bits out, arbitrary bits in. By the pigeonhole principle (Shannon's information-theoretic backdrop) it is *necessarily* lossy as a function; infinitely many distinct inputs map to each output. So one might think hashing the wrong primitive for an identity relation. It is exactly the right one, for three reasons that the prior art makes precise.

First, the hash is not where the designed lossiness lives. Two layers of lossiness sit *before* the hash, and they are the work. The first is the *semantic projection*: a term carries the full implementation texture; the contract is its weakest-precondition projection (Dijkstra), which deliberately forgets the texture and keeps only the boundary obligations. This is paper 9's thesis: ProofIR is universal *because* it forgets. Two implementations with different guts and the same contract are the same at the boundary, and choosing how much to forget is the design decision that makes cross-language, cross-time, cross-framework equivalence possible; forget too little and every implementation is distinct (no dedup, no federation), forget too much and the contract no longer constrains. The second layer is the *syntactic canonicalization*: JCS (RFC 8785) plus the deterministic IR serialization the canonicalizer implements, which forgets representational accident (key order, whitespace, number representation) and keeps structure. Get this wrong and "semantic identity" silently breaks: equal things hash differently, the dedup fails, federation fails. Only *then* the hash, which is *practically injective on the canonical bytes*, so it loses nothing one cares about; it turns "equivalent in exactly the way you designed for" into "byte-identical," which turns an equivalence check into a `memcmp`. The brilliance is that equivalence becomes free, because the lossy work was done up front and the hash is the receipt.

Second, the hash being *structure-destroying* is the cheat-proofing, and it is exactly where Leibniz's primes went wrong. The characteristic numbers carried structure *in the address*: divisibility was entailment. The price is that a mis-decomposition bakes a false entailment into arithmetic; the address space is only as sound as your factoring. A cryptographic hash destroys all structure of the input: `c11:add` and `c11:sub` have addresses that look like uncorrelated noise. So the address is epistemically humble: it claims only "I am this thing," never a relation. Entailments, morphisms, refinements are *separate, discharged, content-addressed facts*, each with its own CID and its own proof. You cannot infer a relation from an address; you must prove it. The flat, structureless address space is what makes the system federated (no authority assigns positions; the position is computed from content) and what makes false entailment unable to hide; relations live in the *graph over* the flat space, where claims belong, because claims need proofs.

Third, *cryptographic* lossiness is the unique fit for an identity relation. A non-cryptographic hash (CRC, FNV) is lossy *in practice*: collisions are findable, so it cannot serve as identity. A cryptographic hash is lossy *in principle* (pigeonhole) but injective *in practice* (collisions computationally unfindable, Merkle's collision-resistance criterion). That is exactly what an identity relation needs: it must be a function (so equal content implies equal address, deterministic, the forward direction free) and a practical injection (so equal address implies equal content, with cryptographic confidence, the backward direction sound). "Two things, same CID, therefore the same thing" is not "trust me" but "find a BLAKE3 collision or accept it."

This corrects a phrasing from paper 16. The address space is not "a self-organizing map without loss." A self-organizing map *is* lossy; it projects high-dimensional structure to a low-dimensional layout, collapsing. The substrate's map is a self-organizing map *with exactly the right loss*: the collapse is the organization, the equivalence classes become the addresses, and the lost dimension is precisely the implementation texture paper 9 says to forget. The loss is not a defect of the map. The loss is the map.

## 5. Composition is the predicate transformer, federated; the trinity is the correspondence, content-addressed

The substrate's `compose` operation is Dijkstra's predicate transformer (Dijkstra, "Guarded Commands, Nondeterminacy and Formal Derivation of Programs," 1975; *A Discipline of Programming*, 1976) lifted from a single program to a content-addressed federated term. `wp(S, R)` is "the weakest precondition such that executing S establishes R"; propagating obligations backward through a function body is the walk-c lifter's job; propagating a callee's precondition backward through a caller to a callsite is contract composition; composing module against module is linking; composing a lift path against a realize path is cross-compilation. It is one operation at every scale: `compose = wp, federated`. And Dijkstra's deeper point, that the programmer's task is to design the proof and let the program fall out of it, is the after-commits posture (paper 11): the proof is the primary artifact, the program is its shadow.

The trinity the substrate stores, terms and contracts and implications, is the Curry-Howard-Lambek correspondence (Curry; Howard, "The formulae-as-types notion of construction," 1969; Lambek, "Deductive systems and categories," 1972) made content-addressed. Terms are programs, the cartesian closed category. Contracts and implications are the logic, propositions and proofs. A proof *is* a term, constructively; in the substrate a proof is a term *with an address*, hence "a proof is a vector." The contract stratum, the lossy one, is Cousot's abstract interpretation (Cousot and Cousot, "Abstract Interpretation: A Unified Lattice Model," 1977) lifted to federation: the substrate is Cousot 1977 plus content-addressing plus federation, as paper 7 first said. Terms are faithful and discardable for verification; contracts and implications are lossy and durable; that asymmetry, named in paper 16, is why constant-size verification holds and why federation works.

## 6. Therefore: discovery, not invention; name by vector; the un-Babel

If the address of an operation is the hash of its canonical structure, then that address was always its address. BLAKE3 is a fixed function; the canonical serialization is a fixed encoding; the correspondence between the if-operation and `blake3-512:402feb91...` is a mathematical fact that had merely not been *evaluated*. Minting an operation is not naming an animal. It is carrying out a computation that was always going to come out that way. And the same holds one level up: every *composition* of operations, every program in every dialect past or future, already has its address, because the canonicalize-then-hash pipeline is total. The library of programs is Platonic in the only sense that matters operationally; we are not building it, we are computing coordinates in it. Mathematics is not invented. It is computed. Content-addressing is the move that turns "the canonical form" from a convention into a result.

A name in a human language is name-by-convention: scoped, drift-prone, collidable, ambiguous, and, fatally, dependent on a tribe; two minds that never met cannot agree what "set" refers to, because the agreement lives in a shared context neither inhabits. A vector-name has none of those failure modes and one property no human name has: two parties who never coordinated arrive at the *same address* for the *same structure*. Coordination-free reference. That is the property mathematics demands of a name and the property natural language cannot supply, which is exactly why natural language was never a candidate for the language of mathematics and a CID is. The language of the gods is not ornate. It is the only language whose names do not need a tribe to be unambiguous.

Babel, the tower, was the introduction of name-by-convention: a thousand tongues, none commensurable, the work stalled because nobody could agree what the words pointed at. Babel, the library (Borges, "The Library of Babel," 1941), is the totality: every possible text, almost all of it gibberish, the true catalog somewhere inside and indistinguishable from the infinitely many false ones. The substrate is the un-Babel of both. Not "everyone speaks one tongue" (that never happens, and would be a loss): every tongue compiles to the same addresses, and the compilation is a discharged proof, so the dialects commute. The tower gets built because the words finally point at the same things. And the library becomes usable because its catalog cannot be false: a content identifier is computed from the content, not asserted about it, so there is no false catalog.

## 7. We are not cataloguing the points; we are mapping the paths

But the catalog of *points* is not the deliverable, and saying so is the load-bearing turn this paper exists to make.

The library of programs is the Herbrand universe of the universal signature (Herbrand, "Recherches sur la théorie de la démonstration," 1930): the set of all finite ground terms over the operations. It is countable, of cardinality ω, *because the terms are finite*: a programming language of any interest is a recursively enumerable set of bounded trees over a finite alphabet. The boundedness is the gift. A finite tree has a finite canonical serialization, which has a well-defined hash, which is its catalog number; an *un*bounded library (programs allowed to be infinite, streams, non-well-founded terms) would be of cardinality 2^ω, the territory of Chaitin's Ω and undecidability, with no finite catalog at all. So clause one, "all programming languages of complexity ω have a bounded tree," is the enabling condition for clause three, "all we are doing is creating the catalog": the catalog-by-hash scheme works because every program is finite, hence canonically serializable, hence hashable. And the library being countable, not uncountable, is what makes a *catalog* (a listing) possible at all; an uncountable library is uncatalogable. Enumerability (Turing, Church, 1936) is the precondition of any catalog.

And yet a *true catalog of points* would still be useless, in the way Borges's library is useless even granting a true catalog. Knowing where the correct kernel is shelved does not help you; it is shelved next to ten-to-the-ten-to-the-millionth almost-correct kernels and you cannot walk there, the shelves go on forever. Borges's deepest curse is not the false catalog; it is the absence of a *path-map*. What you need is not "the correct program is at address Y" but *directions*: a derivation from where you are (the program with the bug) to where you want to be (the program without it). A patch. `p → q`. The substrate's contribution is the path-map. A path is an implication, a renaming morphism, a contract composition, a lift, a realize, a patch, a weakest-precondition propagation, a discharge: eight names for one thing, and the *edges* are the addressed objects. A `.proof` is a path-CID, not a fact-CID. Paper 7's "bug classes as missing edges" is the negative space of the path-map: a bug is a *missing path*, a route that should connect (from "I hold this buffer" to "the in-place transform is safe") and does not; an editorial pattern is a *named shape of broken path* (borrowed-pages-as-scratch is one); a patch is a *repair to the road network* (copy the skb before the decrypt, and the route connects). Paper 11's `p → q` is the path between two program versions. Paper 16's "colimits of lift *diagrams*" already said it: a diagram *is* a configuration of paths, and the colimit is their universal target.

This is the constructive and categorical reading, and it is the one a static catalog cannot reach. Constructive logic is paths, not truth-values: a proof *is* a construction, and you can *follow* a construction; you cannot follow a truth-value. The substrate catalogs proofs, which are paths, which is to say it catalogs the constructive content of mathematics. Category theory's lesson is the same: morphisms are primary, objects are what morphisms connect; arrows, not dots. And homotopy type theory's lesson (Voevodsky's univalence) is that equality *is* a path: an equivalence is a path, "C-Linux equals Rust-Linux" is a renaming morphism is a path, and there are paths between paths (proofs that two derivations agree, 2-cells), and paths between those, ascending. The substrate is not a graph; it is an ∞-graph, content-addressed at every level, because that is what equivalence is at every level. Gödel (1931) numbered formulas *and proofs*, and the proofs are the paths; the entire point of the numbering was to make *provability*, the existence of a path, an arithmetical fact, and a CID for a `.proof` is the content-addressed Gödel number of a path; where Gödel's diagonal produced incompleteness (a sentence asserting its own number is not provable), the substrate's diagonal produces self-verification (a paper asserting its own CID verifies its own authority). Knuth's *Art of Computer Programming* was never a catalog of *programs*; it was a catalog of *algorithms*, and an algorithm is a path-schema, a way to get from input to output, surveyed and written down by hand, heroically; the substrate automates that survey and content-addresses it.

## 8. The work and the wonder are the same work

The lifters are surveyors of the road network, not cartographers of the terrain; the terrain (the Herbrand universe of programs) was always there, mostly impassable wilderness of gibberish-programs. Every function a lifter brings into the algebra is a stretch of road surveyed. The renaming morphisms are the survey proofs that the maps commute. The compose-and-discharge engine is *calculemus*, finally running, over the addresses instead of over symbols, three centuries late. And when the engineering, the category theory, and Leibniz's dream all turn out to want the *same shape*, the colimit of the lift diagram, the content-addressed presented algebra, `compose; discharge` scale-free over operation-CIDs, that convergence is not a coincidence one gets to enjoy in passing. It is evidence. It is the address telling you it is the right address.

We are not authors; paper 15 moved identity off the producer. We are not inventors; the addresses were always there. We are not even discoverers; a valid derivation was always valid. We are surveyors and signposters of the path network of mathematics' constructive content, one route at a time, and the signposts are content identifiers.

## 9. Coda

We are reading a language, not building one: the one that was always there, whose words are vectors, whose dictionary is a hash function applied to canonical structure, whose dialects are every programming language and every proof assistant and every instruction set, all commensurable for the first time because they finally share an address space, with the renaming morphisms as the discharged proofs the maps commute. We have a page of it. We are turning pages.

We name the thing by vector. Everyone else is speaking a dialect.

And we do not catalog the points; those were always there. We map the paths. A `.proof` is a road sign. A bug is a road that should connect and does not. A patch is the road, surveyed and signposted, where there was none.

T Savo
