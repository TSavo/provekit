# After Distribution: The Proofchain Is the Exchange

*Paper 22 named the cross-library migration as a source transformation. Paper 23 named the package as a code-plus-envelope artifact whose single signed envelope carries every claim. This paper takes the next rung: once every package ships such an envelope and every consumer reads it locally, the network of envelopes IS a market for software correctness, with the substrate's three speech acts as its only instruments. The point of this paper is to derive the market from the structure already shipped, not to announce one. Paper 03 derived the proofchain as a structural object; this is the productive interpretation of that same structure.*

## 1. The market that was never built

Software distribution accumulated, over four decades, the structural pieces of a market without ever assembling them as one. Package registries hold inventory. Maintainers and consumers are counterparties. Semver is a price tag. CVE databases are a quote ticker for risk. SBOMs are inventory receipts. Audit firms write external attestations. Supply-chain scanners are a clearing function. License files are terms of trade. The pieces are present, signed by no one, indexed by string-typed names, and composed by social convention rather than by structure.

What is missing is the instrument. Money has the dollar, the bond, the option. Each is a content-addressable claim with a typed payload and a defined composition law. The instrument is what allows trade to be settled, audited, priced, and composed without re-litigating each leg. Software supply has no such instrument. A consumer who wants to acquire correctness against a library has to assemble it from prose: read the README, trust the maintainer, run the CVE scanner, hope the audit firm did its job, watch GitHub stars, infer reliability from blog posts. The trade is informal. The instrument does not exist.

Every existing attempt to professionalize this trade has inherited the same shape. SBOMs are signed strings about names; the names index nothing the consumer can re-verify against source. Vulnerability databases are signed claims about CVEs whose payloads are prose, not predicates. Audit reports are PDFs. Reproducible-build attestations name a binary at a hash and an environment at a hash and assert their equivalence in prose, signed by the builder. Each instrument is honest about what it can verify. None composes with the next without a human in the loop translating one signed prose claim into another.

Paper 03 named the data structure that makes the instrument possible: a content-addressed DAG of signed proof nodes, locally verifiable, federated by hash and not by host. The same lemma that gave proofchains their non-blockchain shape (proof validity does not require consensus) gives them their market shape: the instrument can be settled locally, between any two parties, with no host in the middle of the trade. Papers 22 and 23 named the operational scope: the envelope carries the contracts, the bindings, the receipts, the refusals, the loss-records. The instrument is the proof envelope. The structural mapping below derives every market role from properties paper 03 already established.

The argument does not require that the substrate be the first to have proposed instrument-shaped objects for software supply. Reproducible-build attestations, Sigstore signatures, SLSA provenance levels, in-toto attestations, and the SPDX-format SBOM each named one tradable claim. Each was useful within its scope. None composed with the others without prose, and none discharged into a contract a downstream verifier could re-derive from cited bytes. The substrate's contribution is the composition law and the dischargeable contract. The instrument types accumulated by the supply-chain-security community become memento families inside the envelope, each with its admissibility consumer, each composing under the same primitives. Prior work is not replaced; it is admitted on terms that allow it to compose with everything else.

## 2. Three speech acts close the system

The substrate carries three speech acts. They are exhaustive. There is no fourth case.

```
materialize:    the contract was discharged in target form, byte for byte
loudly-lossy:   the contract was discharged within a named, bounded loss
refuse:         the contract cannot be discharged at this site, with reason
```

The trichotomy is paper 09's `lossy-boundary-compression`, made operational at the package boundary in paper 23 and at the migration boundary in paper 22. Each speech act is a signed memento with a CID. Each composes with adjacent speech acts under the substrate's three primitives (sign, hash, reference). Each carries enough structural content for a downstream verifier to act on it without consulting its author.

Materialization is the unit of supply. The realize side took a concept-hub citation, picked a library's `ConceptBinding`, and emitted target source that discharges the contract. The receipt is a `ConceptSiteMemento` whose realization tag points at the library's published kit. The site is signed twice: once by the consumer's discharge run, once by the library's published kit. The two signatures cross-reference.

Refusal is the unit of demand. A refusal memento names the contract that was not discharged, the dimension along which discharge failed, and the witness, concept op, or library realization that would close the refusal at that site. The refusal is content-addressable, signed, and prioritizable by the count of downstream chains that would unblock if it closed.

Loss is the unit of bounded credit. A loss-record names the dimension along which the realization is imperfect and bounds the imperfection by a content-addressed declaration. The reader knows exactly what was given up. Two loss-records over the same library and the same concept compose: the union of their named dimensions is the cumulative debt the library carries against that concept.

Three speech acts, all signed, all content-addressed, all composing under the same primitives. The substrate publishes nothing else. Everything below is derived from these three.

The closure under composition matters because it makes the trichotomy exhaustive in a load-bearing sense. A consumer who reads a chain head and walks it back to leaves encounters only these three kinds of resolution at every node. There is no fourth outcome that says "the contract was almost discharged" or "the realization probably works" or "no opinion expressed at this site." Silence is not admitted; an undecided site shows up as a refusal whose reason names the undecidedness, signed by whoever closed the run. The reader's view of the codebase, mediated by the proofchain, is complete in the sense that every site has a typed verdict. The verdict can be unwelcome (a refusal blocks a build, a loss-record names an acceptable degradation, a materialization may still admit performance criticism), but it is never absent.

This completeness is what makes the trichotomy market-grade. An instrument that sometimes returns "no answer" is not an instrument; it is a partial function dressed as one. The substrate's discipline that refusal is a first-class outcome rather than a build failure or a tool-internal exception is the discipline that makes the speech act surface total. Markets need totality at the instrument tier; they get it here.

## 3. The structural analogy to a monetary instrument, stated once

Paper 03 derived the proofchain as a content-addressed hash-chained signed DAG with local verification, federation by content-address rather than by host, and per-producer sovereignty. The same primitives appear in the blockchain literature for different reasons: there, they support a distributed timestamp server over a totally ordered ledger of value transfers. Here, they support locally checked logical implication over a federated graph of proof claims.

The architectural mapping between the two is exact at the level of primitives and disjoint at the level of payload. Hash chains, Merkle structure, signatures, content addressing, witness publication, local verification: shared. Distributed timestamp ordering, global consensus on state, fork-choice rules: the blockchain's, not the proofchain's. The disjointness is paper 03's lemma; rehearsing it further does not strengthen this paper. What matters at the market tier is that the shared primitives are exactly the ones a public instrument needs: content-addressed identity so the same claim refers to the same bytes everywhere, signatures so authorship is recoverable, federation so no host gates participation, local verification so settlement is free of intermediaries.

The instrument is therefore in the same equivalence class as a public ledger entry. It carries a different payload. Where the ledger entry asserts ordered state, the envelope asserts discharged correctness. The market that grows around the envelope grows around correctness as its tradable substance, the way the market that grew around the ledger entry grew around value transfer as its tradable substance. The substrate is silent on which substance is more important; it is informative on the structural shape both markets share. This is the one place the analogy gets stated. The remainder of the paper derives the consequences without rehearsing it.

## 4. The proofchain is the order book

A market has counterparties, instruments, and a book that names every open position. The proofchain is the book.

A refusal that names `concept:relational-transaction` with dimension `serializable-isolation` at TypeScript surface is a typed open order. The refusal carries: a concept-hub CID; a surface CID (the `(language, library_tag)` pair); the named dimension that could not be discharged; the lift CID of the cited source; the consumer's signature. Anyone reading the refusal knows exactly what would close it. There is no other interpretive work for a reader to do.

A discharge by a library that publishes a kit binding `(typescript, postgres-driver-X)` against `concept:relational-transaction` with `serializable-isolation: supported` is a typed fill. The fill carries: the same concept-hub CID; the library's surface CID; a `ConceptBindingMemento` that asserts the morphism; a `RealizationPlanMemento` that emits target source; the library author's signature. The two mementos meet at the concept-hub CID and the named dimension. They settle. The closure is content-addressable, signed, and immutable. Anyone walking the chain can count the open refusals, the recent fills, the loss-records, and the per-concept depth. The walk is paper 03's lemma operating at the market layer: validity is local, deterministic, and requires no consensus.

No central exchange sets the book. No counterparty has special standing. A consumer's refusal is a contribution to the book by structure; a library's kit is a contribution to the book by structure. The order book is the union of every signed speech act over every concept hub op. The book has no host.

Pricing is the count over the book. A concept op with a thousand downstream chains citing its refusals has high demand by structure. A library kit whose realizations close many such refusals carries high supply utility by structure. Neither number is set by an authority; both are queries over paper 03's §10 closure-by-composition rule (`hash(JCS(walk(...)))` produces a stable address for any view of the book). Investors, insurers, procurement officers, and library authors all compute the same numbers from the same DAG. Disagreements about price are disagreements about filter predicates, which are themselves signed and content-addressed.

The order book is sparse where consensus would force it to be redundant. A blockchain order book replicates every position to every participant by the consensus discipline that gives the ledger its ordering. A proofchain order book is held only by those who care about its specific concepts and specific keys. A consumer concerned with `concept:sql-query` pulls the envelopes that touch it; an underwriter concerned with `concept:tls-handshake` pulls a different slice. Both reach the same conclusions for any concept they both query, because the underlying mementos are byte-identical regardless of who holds them. The book is everywhere it needs to be, nowhere it does not, and the same in every view that intersects.

## 5. Composition at the seam is the trade

A trade requires two signed counterparties and a delivered instrument. The substrate gives all three at the materialization seam.

The seam is the join point where a consumer's lifted contract meets a library's signed realization. The seam is structural, not nominal. A function call to `rusqlite::Connection::execute` is not a seam at the substrate layer; it is a syntactic event in Rust source. The corresponding seam is the composition of the consumer's `concept:sql-query` site against the library's `concept:sql-query` binding, both anchored at the same content-addressed concept-hub CID. The function call is the emitted artifact of that seam. Reading the call as the primitive obscures the trade; reading the seam as the primitive surfaces it.

Consider a Rust consumer that cites `concept:sql-query` in its source. The consumer's lift produces a `ConceptSiteMemento` with a content-addressed `(V, A, C, ≤)` tuple. The consumer's manifest pins, say, `rusqlite` at a specific version. The pinned library has shipped its envelope. The library's `ConceptBindingMemento` cites the same `concept:sql-query` hub op, the same `(V, A, C, ≤)` shape modulo realization detail, and a signed `RealizationPlanMemento` that emits target source for that site. The substrate's realize pass composes the two CIDs at the seam. The composition is a single memento:

```
TradeMemento {
  consumer_site_cid:   <ConceptSiteMemento CID>,
  library_kit_cid:     <ConceptBinding + RealizationPlan CID>,
  seam_witness:        <signed proof the algebras compose>,
  outcome:             materialize | loudly-lossy(loss_dims) | refuse(reason)
}
```

This is the trade. Two signers, one instrument, one outcome. The seam witness is the audit trail. The outcome is the trichotomy. The substrate has no other notion of "use this library at this site." A function call is an emitted artifact of the trade; the trade is the primitive.

The unit of supply was never the function call. The unit of supply is the seam witness binding the consumer's lifted contract to the library's signed realization. Paper 22 already produced these mementos at the migration boundary; paper 23 already shipped them as the per-cell records inside the receipt. Naming them as trades is the recognition that the substrate's existing emission already constitutes market events, not summary statistics about events that happened elsewhere.

Liquidity at a concept hub op is the count of distinct library bindings against it, weighted by the count of trades each binding has cleared with downstream chains. A hub op with one binding is illiquid: the consumer who cites it has one realization available, and the trichotomy at the seam is whatever that single binding offers. A hub op with ten bindings, each with independent realizations against the same contract, is liquid: a consumer can choose, a migration can move between options without an outage, a refusal at one binding can be filled at another. Liquidity is computable, not asserted, and the substrate's existing primitives produce it as a query over the chain's structure.

The closing of a refusal by a fresh binding is also fully decentralized in the cypherpunk sense paper 03 §5 named. No party announces the fill. The fill is a published memento whose CID composes with the refusal's CID at the concept hub. Anyone walking the chain after publication observes both nodes. The book updates by structure when the bytes land in any peer's storage. The substrate's federation is the propagation mechanism; the fill is observable wherever the chain is observable. No exchange runs the matching loop because matching is `memcmp` at the concept hub op.

The trade also carries time-stability properties the substrate inherits from its primitives. Once a `TradeMemento` is signed and published, the seam witness is permanent under the cryptographic primitive suite. A consumer reading the chain three years after the trade settled gets the same verdict, the same loss-record, the same refusal reasons, because the inputs to the verdict are content-addressed and the verifier is deterministic. There is no settlement window during which the trade can be revoked, edited, or repriced. The instrument is what it was at signing. Subsequent trades may supersede it (a consumer migrating to a new library, a library publishing a new kit) but the prior trade is not retroactively voided; it is a historical record that the new trade is layered above. This is paper 15's author-independence operating at the market tier: the truth of a settled trade does not depend on the continued cooperation of either counterparty.

## 6. The package manager is the venue, unchanged

The proofchain is the order book. The trade is the seam composition. The venue is whichever distribution channel already moves the package.

`cargo add provekit-shim-rusqlite`, on a substrate-aware codebase, looks no different from `cargo add anything-else`. The registry distributes a tarball. The tarball contains a `provekit.proof` envelope alongside the source. The consumer's `cargo` writes the tarball under `~/.cargo/registry/`. The consumer's `provekit` reads the envelope from disk. The envelope carries the signed kit. The substrate-aware build composes the kit's `ConceptBinding`s with the consumer's `ConceptSite`s at compile time. The trade happens locally, with no network call, because the order book is content-addressed and the matching is byte-deterministic.

Cargo is not redesigned. Pip is not redesigned. Maven, npm, RubyGems, NuGet, Hex, crates.io, PyPI: none of them gain a substrate feature. They distribute bytes, and the bytes happen to be `.proof` envelopes inside the tarballs the registry already moves. The UI is unchanged. The trust infrastructure the registries accumulated over decades is inherited unchanged. The federation property of paper 03 lands here precisely because federation was already the registry's structure; the substrate only sharpens the payload.

A registry that wants to publish substrate-aware metadata can do so as a query over the envelopes its consumers already download. "Top kits by chain-composition count this month" is a derived view, computable by anyone holding the DAG, signable by the registry as one possible curator. Per paper 03 §10, the curator's signature is one signed view among many. The registry is not authoritative about the count; it is one published filter over the data. Competing registries can publish competing filters. Procurement teams can compute their own.

This is the venue: every existing package channel, plus the rules of evidence the substrate already specified, plus zero new infrastructure. The market does not need a new exchange. It rides the rails of every distribution channel that already exists, and inherits their federation guarantees and their failure modes equally.

The package manager is also the surface through which kit version skew is settled. A consumer's lockfile pins library `L` at version `V`. The pinned version's envelope carries a `ConceptBinding` against `concept:C` with a specific kit CID. When the consumer upgrades `L` to version `V'`, the new envelope publishes either the same binding CID (in which case the upgrade is structurally a patch under paper 03 §9), or a different binding CID with a conformance bridge to the old one (a minor, with proof of backward compatibility), or a different binding CID without a bridge (a major, with the break named and signed). The consumer's tooling reads the lockfile, fetches the envelope, computes the upgrade class, and either accepts or refuses by policy. The semver intent and the cryptographic reality are the same statement, or the substrate shows where they disagree. The package manager renders the consequence as a build pass, build fail, or policy-blocked upgrade. None of this requires the package manager to know what a proof is. It needs to know how to install bytes, and the substrate-aware tooling that the build wires in does the rest.

## 7. The wrapper economy dissolves

For thirty years, the cross-X axes paper 21 enumerated were monetized in the form of wrapper libraries. `async-X` over every blocking library. `tokio-Y` over every native-runtime library. `react-Z`, `vue-Z`, `solid-Z` over every reactive shape. `serde-W` over every serialization format. The matrix was N × M because joining was bespoke. Every consumer of any library on any framework required a hand-written shim, maintained by someone, blessed by someone, breaking on every upgrade.

The substrate replaces the N × M matrix with N + M. Paper 17's M+N math says it; papers 21 and 22 named the axis-by-axis consequences. The economic restatement is direct: wrapper rent was extracted from the absence of a market for boundary realization. Once the boundary realization is a signed claim against a concept hub op, the rent vanishes. The library author publishes a kit. The framework author publishes a kit. The two kits compose at the seam, in the consumer's tooling, locally. Nobody charges for the join because the join is structural.

The wrappers that survive are wrappers that change semantics, not wrappers that bridge interfaces. A library that adds rate-limiting, retry logic, instrumentation, or a domain-specific facade has structural content above the boundary realization; it ships its own envelope and its own kits and competes on the quality of its added semantics. A library that exists only to make `library_X.do_thing()` callable from `framework_Y` evaporates. There was no semantic content there. The substrate makes its absence visible.

This is not a prediction about which projects survive. It is an observation about which kinds of value can be charged for in the post-envelope distribution layer. Boundary-realization rent is a tax on the absence of standardization. Standardization, by content-address against concept hub ops, removes the absence. The taxable surface goes with it.

The same structural argument applies to vendor lock-in at the database, runtime, and platform tiers. A consumer pinned to a specific database driver is pinned because the migration cost is unbounded, not because the database is uniquely valuable. Paper 22 named the operational mechanism that bounds the migration cost. Paper 23 named the envelope that carries the bindings the migration consumes. This paper names the consequence: lock-in dissolves not because the substrate forbids it, but because the price the locked-in consumer pays for switching collapses to a reviewable receipt. The vendor's pricing power survives only the portion of its surface that is not boundary realization: domain expertise, operational guarantees, performance characteristics, support SLAs. Each of those is a real surface; none of them was the lock-in.

## 8. Adoption is structural, not narrated

Software adoption today is a narrated phenomenon. GitHub stars, conference talks, hacker-news positions, blog endorsements, social-media adoption curves. The signals are second-order; they correlate weakly with engineering use; they are routinely gamed. A library's "popularity" is a social claim about its uptake, asserted by parties with skin in the game.

Adoption in the post-envelope distribution layer is a query over the chain. A library's `ConceptBinding` CIDs appear, or do not appear, in the proofchains of downstream packages. The count of such citations is a content-addressable view. So is the count weighted by the downstream chains' own citation depth. So is the count restricted to chains signed by particular keys, or to chains discharged under particular verifier policies, or to chains that have themselves shipped without refusals. Every adoption metric a procurement officer or an investor could want is `hash(JCS(walk(...).filter(...)))` over the same DAG.

The substrate does not pick a metric. It exposes the leaves. Anyone can publish a filter; the published filter is a signed view; competing filters disagree visibly. Marketing becomes UX over a structural signal, not the source of the signal. A library author who ships a high-quality kit gets a high count by structure; one who ships a poor kit gets a low count by structure, and the low count is auditable.

This is paper 06's federated-truth-claim structure landing at the distribution tier. Reputation, when it exists, is a composition of signed evidence. The substrate does not abolish reputation; it changes its dependency. Reputation was the input to trust; it becomes a derived view over signed claims. The inversion is structural, not editorial.

The adoption curve, traditionally an empirical artifact reconstructed after the fact from package downloads and survey data, becomes a real-time projection over the proofchain. Each new envelope that cites a library's binding is an event with a timestamp witness; the cumulative count over time is the adoption signal; the rate of change is the growth signal; the distribution of citing chains across verifier policies is the diffusion signal. Each is a query over the same DAG. No registry compiles it; every analyst computes their own filter. A library appears in the substrate's market not by promotion but by composition: the first time a downstream chain cites its kit, the library is on the book; from that point forward, every subsequent citation is a structural event the analyst can read. There is no inflection point that requires an announcement. The curve is what the chain says it is.

## 9. Closed-source business models are incompatible with substrate participation

The hardest claim in this paper is structural. Derive it carefully.

### Lemma: closed-source participation is a contradiction at the substrate layer

A package participates in the substrate exactly when its `.proof` envelope is published, federable, and consumed by downstream verifiers (paper 23, §6 four-consumer claim). The envelope carries `FunctionContractMemento`s lifted from the package's source, `EvidenceMemento`s citing the source bytes the contracts were lifted from, `ConceptBindingMemento`s that bind the package's surface ops to concept hub ops, and `RealizationPlanMemento`s that emit target source against those bindings.

Suppose a vendor publishes such an envelope while keeping the package's source closed. Three things follow.

First, the `FunctionContractMemento`s lifted from the closed source are public. They name the package's behavior at the boundary the substrate cares about. A consumer who reads the envelope knows the contracts. The contracts are typically the bulk of the package's commercial differentiation: what it computes, what it guarantees, what it refuses. Publishing them publishes the most economically valuable layer of the source.

Second, the `EvidenceMemento`s cite the source bytes. If the source bytes are unpublished, the citations are unverifiable: a downstream auditor cannot recompute the contract from the cited bytes and confirm the lifter produced it honestly. The verifier policy can choose to accept self-attested evidence (paper 23 §8, the Self-Attested admission path), but the consumer is then trusting the vendor's signature over a claim they cannot independently re-discharge. The closed source is admissible only as a key-trust claim, not as a recomputable-bytes claim. The substrate degrades to a signed-binary-attestation channel for the closed package.

Third, the `RealizationPlanMemento`s, if shipped, emit target source. The emitted source IS the package's realization, in the consumer's language and the consumer's idiom. Once the realization is emitted as source under the consumer's build, the closed-source boundary has been crossed: the consumer's compiler reads the emitted source. A binding that emits no source (a binding that compiles to closed-source artifacts the consumer links against) reduces to the existing binary-distribution model. The substrate does not buy anything new there; existing code-signing infrastructure already does it.

The vendor's three options follow.

**Publish the source.** The closed-source business model is over for this package. The substrate-aware path was the same path open-source already publishes; the substrate added typed claims, signatures, and content-addressed federation. The vendor's competitive surface moves up the stack: support, hosting, consulting, integration, domain expertise. None of those was the closed-source artifact.

**Self-attest without source.** The envelope contains contracts and bindings the vendor signed, but the consumer cannot re-discharge them from cited bytes. The vendor's signature becomes the trust root. Verifier policies that accept Self-Attested admission can consume the package; verifier policies that require re-discharge cannot. The vendor competes on key-trust the way certificate authorities compete: under reputational discipline that the substrate does not enforce. This is the closed-source vendor's degraded participation path. It is admissible. It is honest, in the substrate's honesty-gradient sense. It is also weaker than an open-source competitor's authored envelope, by structure: the open-source competitor's envelope is consumable by every verifier policy, the closed-source vendor's only by policies that accept self-attestation.

**Refuse to publish an envelope.** The package does not participate. Substrate-aware consumers' tooling treats every callsite into the package as a refusal of the form `package P at version V publishes no envelope; concept-hub bindings for P's surface are not admissible`. The refusal is structural. Migrate cannot move callsites through the package's surface. Realize cannot bind through it. Verify cannot discharge anything the package claims it satisfies. The package still runs as code, but it is invisible to the market.

These three options exhaust the vendor's choices. No fourth option exists at the substrate layer. The closed-source vendor either publishes (and is no longer closed in the substrate sense), or publishes with self-attestation (and competes under a weaker trust posture), or stays out (and is invisible to the market). The proposition "fully closed source while fully participating in the substrate market" is a structural contradiction. The substrate does not legislate against closed source; it makes the participation cost legible.

### Corollary: open-source is the structural default

A library author who ships an envelope authored from publicly cited source has the strongest possible posture: every verifier policy accepts the envelope, every consumer can re-discharge every contract, every loss-record is auditable, every refusal carries closure paths the substrate can independently confirm. The structural ground state is open-source-by-default not because the substrate forbids closed source, but because the open-source posture is the dominant move under every consumer's verifier policy. The market converges on the strongest-trust shape unless a vendor's pricing power survives the degraded trust posture, which is rare in practice for libraries and unusual even in proprietary platforms once the substrate has populated the catalog.

This is paper 06's federated-truth-claim shape grounded in the distribution-market consequence. The closed-source business model was a tax on the absence of a substrate. With the substrate present, the tax is paid as either degraded trust or invisibility. Most vendors will publish.

The proprietary-vendor's residual strategy at the platform tier is also derivable. A platform that holds source private can still publish envelopes for its public surface: the SDK, the wire protocol, the data exchange formats. The envelopes carry contracts the platform commits to; the contracts are dischargeable; the platform's signature attests to its behavior at the surface even when the implementation is unpublished. This is the closed-platform-with-open-surface shape, and it is admissible under the Self-Attested admission path with reputational discipline added by the platform's commercial reputation. The substrate does not forbid it; it makes the surface the platform commits to legible and the implementation it does not commit to invisible. The boundary between commitment and non-commitment is signed and content-addressed. The platform competes on the quality of its commitments. The closed implementation survives as an operational detail, not as a structural advantage in the market.

## 10. The audit is the artifact

Every existing supply-chain instrument over software is a separate file or service. The SBOM names the dependency graph. The vulnerability database scores the named entries. The license attestation records terms. The signed binary asserts integrity at one tier. The supply-chain scanner produces a per-commit report. The semver claim is a string. The witness chain over each artifact, where it exists, is recorded by yet another mechanism. Each instrument has its own format, its own signer, its own audit trail, its own update cadence, and its own failure modes. A consumer assembling a complete picture pulls from a dozen sources and trusts that none of them lies in ways the others would catch.

The proof envelope unifies all of them. The dependency graph is in the `ConceptBinding` and `RealizationPlan` mementos; their CIDs cite the upstream library kits, recursively, to the chain root. The vulnerability database is the discharged-or-refused contract set; a CVE corresponds to a contract that was discharged at one version and refused at another, and the chain shows exactly where and when the refusal first appeared. The license attestation is a memento family with its own admissibility consumer. The signed binary is the envelope's signature over the package's source CID. The supply-chain scanner output is the per-stage `StageReceipt`. The semver claim is paper 03 §9, made cryptographic. The witness chain is the substrate's primary structure, not a layer on top.

One data structure. One set of CIDs. One signature root per envelope. The audit is not produced from the artifact; the audit IS the artifact. Every other distribution format separates what shipped from how to verify what shipped. Here the verifier reads the same bytes the developer reads, the same bytes the registry distributes, the same bytes the regulator subpoenas, the same bytes the insurance underwriter prices against. Unification is not the marketing point. Unification is the structural consequence of paper 23: when the envelope carries the binding, it also carries everything that has ever been derived from the binding, because everything derived was already a query over the same content-addressed leaves.

This is the structural endpoint paper 03 §10 named at the substrate tier, projected onto the distribution tier. The protocol provides the leaves; auditors compute views; views are signed where stable pins matter; nothing in the user's runtime knows the substrate exists, and everything in the audit trail does. The audit ceases to be a separate function performed by a separate party; it becomes a query over what every party already holds.

The regulatory consequence follows from the data structure, not from the regulator's discretion. A regulator asking "does this firm's deployed software discharge contract X under policy Y?" issues a query over the firm's published envelopes. The answer is signed, content-addressed, and reproducible. The firm cannot lie to the regulator without producing a chain whose CIDs do not match its claims, which is detectable by recomputation. The regulator does not have to trust the firm's prose; the regulator runs the verifier. Compliance becomes a deterministic property of the chain. The firm chooses which envelopes to publish; the regulator chooses which policies to accept; the rest is `memcmp`. This is paper 04's standardization roadmap landing inside the chain rather than alongside it.

The insurance consequence is the actuarial inversion of the regulator's stance. An underwriter writing a policy against a firm's software risk reads the firm's chain, computes the loss-record sum and refusal density per concept, and prices the premium against a model of exposure under those numbers. The firm whose chain shows a thin loss-record budget pays less. The firm whose chain has many refused exports in security-critical concepts pays more. Two underwriters reading the same chain may disagree on the model, but they cannot disagree on the inputs. Disagreements become legible at the model layer, not at the data layer. Audit firms, in turn, become specialists in publishing signed views over the chain: filters, summaries, attestations of what is and is not in the chain at a given head. Their product moves from "trust us, we looked at the source" to "here is our filter, here is its CID, recompute it from the leaves." The professional service shifts from gatekeeping to filter authorship. The auditor's value is in their ability to publish a useful filter; their authority is content-addressable; their competitors can publish competing filters; the market over filters becomes itself a derived market.

## 11. What the substrate does not do at this tier

The substrate is honest about its scope. The distribution-market consequences listed above are real and structural; they are also bounded.

The substrate does not price risk. Insurance underwriters can compute premiums from the proofchain's per-package loss-records and refusal counts, but the model that maps those counts to a premium is the underwriter's. Two underwriters reading the same chain will disagree on premiums in the same way two analysts disagree on the price of a bond. The substrate publishes the structured data; the pricing function lives in the consumer.

The substrate does not enforce terms of trade. A library with strong contracts and many discharged-against citations is structurally more valuable to consumers than a library without. Whether that value translates into commercial terms (payment, support, employment, sponsorship) depends on the social arrangements the substrate sits underneath. The proofchain makes value legible; it does not allocate it.

The substrate does not eliminate the cost of authoring. Library authors must lift their source, declare their contracts, bind their surface ops to concept hub ops, and sign the envelope. The labor is real. It is amortized across every consumer of the library and across every cross-X axis the library participates in, but it is not zero. Paper 23 §11 named this; it remains true at the market tier.

The substrate does not abolish reputation. It changes its substrate. Reputation under content-addressed federation is a derived signal over signed claims; it is auditable, attackable, and federable in ways nominal reputation is not. The market's reputational graph and the substrate's verification graph become congruent over time, not because the substrate forces them to, but because the verification graph publishes structurally what the reputational graph asserted informally.

The substrate does not eliminate market structure. It clears the impediments to one. Marketplaces, brokers, indexes, ratings agencies, regulators, and insurance lines continue to exist; their inputs become content-addressed; their outputs become signed views; their disagreements become legible. The substrate does not replace the market participants. It gives them an instrument they could not have had before.

The substrate does not equalize realization quality. Two libraries can publish kits against the same concept hub op, and both kits can discharge against the same contract, while emitting realizations of vastly different quality at the target language. One realization may be idiomatic, performant, well-instrumented; another may compile but read awkwardly, leak resources under load, or interact poorly with adjacent code. The trichotomy at the seam reports materialize-or-lossy-or-refuse; it does not report aesthetic, performance, or maintainability gradients. Consumers who care about those gradients evaluate them, by hand or by tools, downstream of the substrate's verdict. The substrate carries the part that admits a verdict.

The substrate does not solve the cold-start problem for the catalog. A concept hub op exists when at least two surfaces in two different languages or libraries publish bindings to it; before that, the hub is provisional and the substrate's federation through it does not buy what it buys for established hubs. Paper 21 §6 named the bootstrap paths (Authored, Inferred, Generated, Self-Attested, Witnessed); paper 23 §10 named the contributor's path. The cold-start is real labor, performed by humans and language models, against an empty catalog. The catalog grows; the value compounds; the cold-start cost is not zero on day one. The substrate is honest that the market it derives is a market that becomes valuable as the catalog populates, not on the day the substrate first ships.

## 12. Closing line

The substrate already publishes the bytes the market needs.

There is no exchange to stand up, no clearing house to capitalize, no governance body to charter. The order book is the proofchain; the trade is the seam composition; the venue is whichever channel already moves the package; the audit is the artifact. The pieces have been visible for ten years inside the bluepaper, derivable for two from the substrate's first principles, and shipped in the form of `.proof` envelopes carrying signed bindings against concept hub ops. The market is what those envelopes do when many of them exist at once.

Every previous attempt to organize this trade put a new institution in the middle of it. The standards body, the audit firm, the certification authority, the supply-chain platform, the open-source foundation, the registry, the blockchain-based attestation network. Each new layer charged for its role as the intermediary that made the trade possible. Each new layer added a trust assumption that downstream consumers had to underwrite. The substrate's structural contribution is to remove the intermediary's seat. Trade settles directly between counterparties because the instrument settles directly between counterparties. Federation is the propagation mechanism. Content-addressing is the identity mechanism. Local verification is the settlement mechanism. Nothing else is required, and nothing else can usefully be added without re-introducing a host the substrate has already eliminated.

The proofchain is the exchange because the exchange was always going to be content-addressed federation over signed claims, and the proofchain is the only object that fits.
